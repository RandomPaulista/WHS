African front (and Arabia)

Tunisian and Tripolitanian Invasions

Tunis and Tripoli Repel Italy

After declaring independence from the Ottomans and seizing local garrisons, the governments of Tunis and Tripolitania turned their attention to the pressing threat: the Italian landings on the North African coast. Rather than pushing into Egypt as the Ottomans demanded, they prioritized expelling the invaders. Italian forces were pushed back into the sea.

Casualties: 7K North Africans; 15K Italians

Tripolitanian-Egyptian Clash

Advance Toward Alexandria

After the repulsion of Italy, Tripolitanian troops advanced eastward into Egyptian territory. With most of Egypt's forces committed in the Levant, the western frontier proved vulnerable. Although Tripolitania claimed much of the borderlands, the Egyptian army managed to stabilize the front near Alexandria.

Casualties: 5K North Africans; 10K Egyptians

Moroccan Advance into Algeria

Tlemcen Falls

The Moroccan army launched an invasion of western Algeria, quickly capturing the city of Tlemcen. The local population, disillusioned with French rule, largely welcomed the Moroccan forces. France, underestimating the scope of the incursion, sent only small garrisons, allowing the Moroccans to entrench themselves with minimal resistance.

Casualties: 1K Moroccans; 1K French

Levantine and Mesopotamian Rebellions

Ottomans and Egyptians Turn Inward

The Ottoman Empire and Egypt, both distracted by external wars, were forced to halt their campaigns as rebellion erupted within their own ranks. Entire units in the Levant and Mesopotamia mutinied, turning their weapons on their commanders and declaring loyalty to the Meccan-led jihad. Both states redirected their forces inward, attempting to suppress the uprisings â€” but the sheer scale and religious fervor of the insurgents made this impossible.

Casualties: 18,000 rebels; 15K Egyptian; 7k Ottomans

Yemen and Nejd's Religious Mobilization

Hejazi Independence

The scholars of Mecca, seeing the Ottomans' reluctance to formally declare jihad, took matters into their own hands. They issued a direct call to arms, framing the war as a spiritual obligation for all Muslims. This declaration galvanized support across the Islamic world, with Yemen and Nejd joining forces to organize a united front against the Egyptians.

Casualties: 0, no armies sent from Egyptian side

Sudanese and Horn of Africa Uprisings

Somalis Lead Rebellion and Invasion

Sufi orders in Sudan and the Horn of Africa declared the war a cleansing of foreign influence. Egypt's betrayal pushed them into full rebellion, leading to widespread uprisings against Egyptian rule. The Sufi leaders framed their struggle as a divine war, rallying support from local tribes and communities. The Somalis interpreted the call to jihad as a chance to expel the Christian Ethiopians from their lands, while Sudanese tribes sought independence from both Ottoman and Egyptian rule.

Casualties: 1k Somali; 1k Ethiopian

Persian Gulf

British Invasion of Kuwait

As British forces prepared to launch an ambitious landing in Kuwait, the region erupted. The Trucial States declared jihad, viewing the British presence as a direct threat to Islam and sovereignty. Oman quickly followed, cutting off vital naval access and choking British logistics in the Gulf.
Though the British forced their way through with superior naval firepower, they suffered heavily. Many vessels were burned or seized along the coast, and soldiers were picked off by coordinated tribal and mujahideen attacks. By the time they reached Kuwait, the exhausted invaders met fierce Mesopotamian resistance, organized and dug in from previous uprisings. The British force was routed. The invasion had failed.
On the flip side, the British managed to secure Persian neutrality and military passage.

Casualties: Entire British landing force eliminated

Zanzibar and the Indian Ocean

Seychelles seized from the British

The British struggled to maintain control over their Indian Ocean territories. The Sultanate of Zanzibar, emboldened by the jihad's call, launched a surprise attack on the Seychelles, seizing the islands with minimal resistance. The British garrison, caught off guard and outnumbered, surrendered quickly. This victory boosted Zanzibar's prestige and influence in the region.

Casualties: 1k Zanzibar 1k British
